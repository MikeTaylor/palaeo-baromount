# Notes on letter transcriptions from the Carnegie Museum archive

This document contains information relavant to the AMNH _Barosaurus_ that was extracted from `Archival letter transcriptions 1889-1932 (Betty Hill).doc`.

There are several mentions of _Barosaurus_, but none from later then 1920 -- all pertaining to other specimens:

> I had told you of the neck, several vertebrae of which we had seen before quitting the quarry in 1912 -- the neck which I supposed belonged to No. 165. We have been taking it out in blocks but not until a short time ago did we discover, or see, two ends of the same vertebra. We were much surprised to find that the centrum didn't lack an inch of being three feet long. He must have been a giraffe-dinosaur. The vertebrae as far as we can see them look very Diplodocus-like. Unfortunately I have not Marsh's monograph here and have to trust to memory. I have wondered if this is not Barosaurus.  
> -- Douglass to Holland, 18 November 1914 (page 1138)

See also: "These elongate cervicals (CM 1198) probably belong to a partial skeleton, field #155 (now ROM 3570), which was originaliy identified as Diplodocus." (McIntosh 2005:42). They are probably now incorporated into the "Gordo" mount.

> What you write about the discovery of the big cervical vertebrae three feet in length is very interesting. In the matter of length these vertebræ suggest that your surmise that they may belong to Marsh’s Barosaurus is correct. But who knows? They may be the vertebræ of Rigg’s Brachiosaurus. The German paleontologists have fallen in with the suggestion which I made a couple of years ago before the Paleontological Society, in a paper which I read, that their big beast is a Brachiosaurus. The cervical vertebræ of Brachiosaurus brancai of East Africa are reported by them as being over four feet in length. “Deutschland über alles!”  
> -- Holland to Douglass, 3 December 191. (page 1142).

(It is news to me that Holland suggested the Tendaguru brachiosaur might be _Brachiosaurus_.)

> I have just received Lull's the Sauropod Dinosaur Barosaurus. I would not be at all surprised if No. 310 -- the specimen with the long neck, of which I have written you many times -- turned out to be Barosaurus. It looks much like it so far as I have seen our specimen. There is the same proportional difference between the lengths of the cervicals and dorsals. He must have been a strangely disproportioned brute. I imagine he pushed his neck along and dragged his tail and his legs were used for propellers and to give room in his body for a square meal which must have consisted principally of hot air, carbon dioxide and water for if his head was like that of Diplodocus it is difficult to see how he could have procured enough of anything else. This is scientific speculation, however, and there has been too much of that already.  
> -- Douglass to Holland, 14 February 1920 (page 1289).

There are however mentions of No. 340, which became AMNH 6241:

> We have been pushing the excavating at the quarry, hoping that we might clear things up to the new Stegosaurus skeleton (No. 350) and the Sauropod Dinosaur (No. 340) and get them out at an early date so that, if nothing else of unusual interest came to light we could discontinue work if it were thought best; but now another skeleton has come to light (No. 345) which apparently lies over the Sauropod (340) and nearly over the Stegosaurus -- perhaps directly over part of it. We do not yet know to shat genus No. 345 belongs or No. 340 for that matter. Of No. 340 we have as yet seen only a large portion of the neck. Part of the cervicals and dorsals of No. 345 are exposed.  
> -- Douglass to Holland, 5 April 1921 (page 1322).

> The skeleton of Diplodocus which must first be taken out is No. 355. At least this lies over the tail of the other specimen of Diplodocus, No. 340-365. The neck of the latter was numbered 340 and the dorsals were supposed to belong to a different individual and were given a different number. We will call the specimen No. 340.  
> [...]  
> The larger portion of the neck of No. 340 has been taken down. The dorsals are present, and probably the tail to the whip. This is also an excellent specimen.
> &nbsp;  
> If Utah should take out No. 355 this would leave No. 340 to be taken out by some other museum or to be left if the government should uncover in relief a portion of the quarry for a permanent exhibit.  
> -- Douglass to Stewart, 20 September 1922

(There are mentions in another letter that I already have: Douglass, Earl. 1922b. Letter to George P. Merrill expanding on cost and time to extract two Diplodocus skeletons from the Dinosaur National Monument quarry. 22 November 1922.)

(And more mentions in Douglass, Earl, 1922c. Report of Work Done by the Carnegie Museum at the Dinosaur National Monument in Uinta County, Utah, under the Supervision of Earl Dougless, in the year 1922. 24 March 1923.)

#365 is mentioned only once more, in Stewart, Douglas. 1922a. Letter to Arthur S. Coggeshall offering to turn over the two Diplodocus skeletons at Dinosaur National Monument quarry to USNM. 6 September 1922. Here it is used to refer to the specimen we know as #340.
